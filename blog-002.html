I have been learning Japanese since August of 2024 and in an attempt to prepare myself
for my trip to Japan I have become substantially more diligent.


This is my current strategy for learning Japanese, which as of right now I think is the best way to learn any language.

You want to keep things quite simple. Here is advice in no particular order, I'm not going to cradle you like a guru. If you want to look at other resources you should, but I have found this to improve my ability the most.

You need an SRS. You can acquire language without it, but you will just suffer. The distribution of natural language is not uniform. As such you need to artificially increase the amount of times you see certain words: you can do this both by re-reading/re-watching material (Which is a must), but you can also do this by using Anki to fascilitate memorizing words.

There are tools that help fascilitate generating flash cards so that you spend less time on writing them down. This is sort of optional, it has some draw backs because the tools are not quite perfect (maybe one day I'll make a pull request in a repository for my style).

Yomitan is one such tool, a browser extension usually used by the Japanese Learning community, it provides a quick lookup for words you find according to a dictionary you import (you can have multiple, both in Japanese or English). It also allows you to click one button and add the word to Anki.
This is honestly fantastic, but the formatting is significantly on the dictionary. It often produces cards that have excessive information and get distracting. I haven't found a better solution, but I'm sure once I can read Japanese definitions consistently this will cese to be an issue.

The more time you spend providing attention to your target language the better.

Don't be afraid of reading, reading is the most important thing you can do, the more you put it off the more time you waste. Reading expands your vocabulary the quickest, you need to be doing it day 1. The other reason to read is because it develops familiarity with how you interact with flash cards, Your flash cards will be in your target language (at least the front side).

Utilize your target language in as many things as you can, write notes (even if digital), set your system language, set your browser settings You want as much exposure as possible.

Depending on your expectations you will have to change the amount of time you are spending with your target language. At a minimum spend an hour a day "immersing" with your target language. This is reading books and website, watching films and shows, as well as listening to podcasts and audio books.

I know I told you that you should look at other people's resources, and you should, but do not fall into the trap of inaction. Your goal is to learn Japanese not learn every possible way to learn Japanese. Language acquisition can be achieved many ways, but You are here to do it in a reasonable amount of time. Don't listen to people that try to eliminate too much nuance, they are salesmen, there might be some nuggets of gold hidden in there, but for the most part they are just looking to make money. There is lots of dogma in the Japanese Lanugage Learning community just try to avoid it. Also don't pay for ANYTHING, you can learn any language for free you don't ever need to pay money for any online lessons or whatever (maybe vocational schools are alright, but I've never been to one *shrug*).

Make sure to learn the pronunciation details of your target language, don't listen to people that tell you it's a total waste of time. It is as important as you want it to be, you will be intelligable by native speakers, but of course you'll have the foreigner's accent. Is this something you're ok with? If it is then just worry about vocabulary; If you are concerned about it, remember that exposure the language will only help those particularities that seperate your abilities from natives. Dedicated study is also important if you want to compensate for your non nativeness. Maybe the Japanese Language Learning community is particular odd about this subject, but pitch accent is important, at least to me. However I don't intentionally study it, at least I don't right now. I find myself in the "I know many words, but I might not pronounce them correctly" camp vs the "I can pronounce a few words but correctly" camp. I am aware that I'll have an accent and I'll fix it eventually, but personally I want to spend as much time Enjoying the language as I can instead of worrying about whether I'm doing things correctly. I know it might become more difficult for me in the future to correct those maneurisms, but enjoyment is the most important thing in language acquisition.

Persistance. Persistance is the most important aspect of this whole thing. If you miss one day- You're fucked-. Well maybe not completely, but this language learning game is very very much based on momentum. Find a pace you can match everyday and stick to it. It might take you years to get where you want to be, but it'll be worth it. If you are using my strategy so far, you have to be in this for the long haul, if you want to casually get good at a language go you Duolingo or whatever, but its for casual usage. It'll help if you visit a country and ask for the bathroom, but if you want to have deep conversation, read literature, and watch shows without much effort you need to make a study out of it and not masterbation.

This is the only true wisdom I have out of all of this. This is advice i have heard no where else, everything up until now has been advice I have absorbed and believed to be true. Do not use premade study decks. Premade study decks waste a lot of time. This goes against the common wisdom, which is you should just use a 1000 or 2000 word premade study deck because most of those words will show up in anything you immerse in. This is mostly correct, but the particular media you are consuming at an immediate moment will not have that average distribution that those decks target. Even if the deck is designed for the most common words in Anime or in Manga, it doesn't matter, because it isn't a deck aimed at Volume 1 of the Chainsaw Man manga. Why do I make such a big deal out of this? Because as a beginner the immediate improvements, and guarentee of those words in your deck being in material you are consuming, I believe, is a huge deal. I spent my entire first year using premade decks and I agonized through them. I was able to acquire a decent amount of vocabulary, but I HATED flashcards. I could not stand them. I still despise them, but much less so, because it takes so much less time to get through them now that I only have cards for words I have seen in my target media. From day one, you should be word mining your target media, whether that's subtitles, your system settings, or a book you are reading: You need to be writing all of those words down and putting them in your SRS. This is honestly the most important advice I have for anyone still stuck on the earlier stages of language learning (not that I think I'm all that deep in). You need to re-read and re-watch the same media a dozen times, and you need those words in your SRS, it is truly that simple. Premade decks ironically probably work better when you have gone through more media, but at that point you already know the words because you have mined them elsewhere.

Reconsuming media is also incredibly important. It artificially increases the number of times you encounter words, but it also increases the ease of your flashcards and increases card retention significantly. Of course the retention stat doesn't matter, that isn't the point, you want to be able to recognize the word in any random context and be able to recall it. You will have "learned" or "acquired" it.

I don't know where I developed this misconception, but watching shows without your target language's subtitles doesn't really do you too much benefit. Sure it develops your ability to listen and parse the language, but you can't look up words nearly as easily (especially languages that derive their systems from classical chinese). Of course if you aren't word mining since the beginning this just leaves you deeper in the dark.

Don't be afraid to speak your target language either, probably the sooner the better. Total immersion time absolutely helps, but interacting with actual humans 100% improves your abilities. Of course this depends on your goals, but speaking is important if you want to live there or have meaningful conversation and not just sit there awkwardly like a brick wall.

I guess that's all the language talk I have in me for now. Good bye.


P.S. The whole "learning" vs "acquisition" thing is some dogma from the Japanese Learning Community, please disregard it. Any normal person will understand what you mean when you say you want to "learn" a language.
